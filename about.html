Iowa City, Iowa

The Handcart Beginning

Ten companies of handcart pioneers walked the 1,300 mile trail from Iowa City, the end of the rail line, to Salt Lake City, Utah between 1856 and 1860. They pulled and pushed all that they owned. Of the 2,962 handcart immigrants, about 250 perished along the way- 220 of them in companies four and five, the Willie and Martin companies of 1856.

"Many a father pulled his cart, with his little children on it, until the day preceding his death" (LeRoy R. Hafen and Ann W. Hafen, Handcarts to Zion: The Story of a Unique Western Migration 1856–1860, [1960], 102).

Thousands of immigrants from England and Wales who joined the Church and the trek west took on a new form of transportation on the journey to Salt Lake City. Most could not afford wagons after leaving their homes, so they pulled handcarts. The human-powered handcarts, envisioned by Brigham Young, proved to be one of the most brilliant—and tragic—experiments in all western migration.

Iowa City was the end for the west-bound railroad in 1856. Here, the convert emigrants were outfitted with handcarts to begin their trek. With practically empty carts, they made good time across Iowa to Council Bluffs. Here they were able to acquire the remaining provisions and supplies for their long march.
